Let me introduce myself

I'm just a Midwestern girl with a Californian home, Chinese roots, Middle Eastern soul, and an African fire burning in my eyes..



Hi, my name is Tiffany Tong. I was born in Milwaukee, WI and have the foam cheesehead to prove it! I am the only child of parents who met in the midwest after immigrating from Hong Kong and Taiwan. I loved Milwaukee but wanted the challenge of life in a big city so headed to UCLA for college. Soon after, my parents moved out to Los Angeles to follow me and the better weather. After graduation, I went straight into the PhD program at Princeton University.


To be completely honest, I was never sure if I really wanted to be an engineer. For a long time, I couldn't reconcile the technical training with my desire to do work that had an inherent global focus, such as international relations or political science. But then I met my future advisor, Prof. Wole Soboyejo, who identified me as a kindred spirit and introduced me to an entire sub-field dedicated to applying technology to solve specific challenges in developing regions. Within two months I found myself in Akure, Nigeria, and finally knew what passion felt like. Working with Prof. Soboyejo allowed me to immerse myself in a number of projects combining science, policy, and entrepreneurship. Many of these projects addressed questions of energy access, particularly in rural off-grid communities in sub-Saharan Africa.


After receiving my PhD in 2012, I moved half-way around the world to join a research center focused on the combination of computer science and development economics. I am primarily driven by work that is both meaningful and intellectually engaging, regardless of the specific technology or sector that it involves. So I embraced the opportunity to learn more about both software development and the agricultural sector. With CTED, I have been able to tackle issues such as designing usable and scalable mobile apps for semi-literate users, assessing needs for end-to-end systems in resource-constrained environments, and understanding how small-scale farmers make their livelihoods.


But as much as I am driven by work that I am passionate about, I also value having time to pursue my hobbies and other interests. I love travel and there are few things that I love more than exploring a new neighborhood, trying a new cuisine, or learning how to laugh in a different language. Through my journeys, I have also cultivated a passion for street photography and music and have showcased a number of my photos and mixtapes on this site. I hope you enjoy and can learn a little bit about me as you browse around!

CTED
The Center for Technology and Economic Development




When computer science meets development economics



In September 2012, I moved to the UAE to join The Center for Technology and Economic Development, a research center based at New York University's campus in Abu Dhabi. This has been an exciting opportunity to work with team members in the US, Middle East, and Africa, and come together to collectively study economic markets and the role of technology in addressing common challenges in developing regions.


My role at CTED has combined elements of both researcher and project manager. The majority of my projects have centered around the development of Android apps to crowdsource high quality data from rural areas in Ghana, ranging from food production to transportation costs. We work mostly with rural farmers and agricultural extension officers and few of our users had ever even operated smartphones before our projects.

A farmer tests out one of our apps

In these environments, it was therefore essential that we develop straightforward applications that took challenges such as semi-literacy, unreliable mobile network infrastructure, and long periods between potential battery charges into account. In many cases, this meant sacrificing trendy features or "stylish" UI designs for practicality. Fonts and buttons had to be enlarged, color schemes had to be visible in bright sunlight, and interactions had to be deliberate to minimize user errors, meaning that even basic swiping gestures had to be avoided. Moreover, data transfer had to be efficient, storage solutions had to accommodate multiple source channels, and visualizations had to suit different users ranging from community members to academic researchers.

A sample UI interface for one of our mobile apps. Simplicity is key.

Following an iterative design process that allowed us to quickly pivot based on our evolving needs, I traveled to Ghana numerous times over a two-year period to conduct background research and field test our apps with several communities. I then worked with our software developers - based in both Abu Dhabi and Accra - to apply lessons from the field and coordinate the development of end-to-end systems that integrate our apps with appropriate data storage and visualization tools.

Collecting drone imagery from our farms

Through the years, I have also led an in-depth research project investigating the accuracy of GPS in smartphones compared with professional surveying equipment, written Espresso tests to automate UI testing of our apps, and introduced a Node JS web tool for improved data verification. I've designed UI layouts, flown drones, and danced azonto in the streets of Accra. In short, I've had the time of my life!



The Solar Lantern Project
OFF-GRID SOLAR |TECHNOLOGY FOR DEVELOPING REGIONS




Over 1 billion people lack reliable access to electricity



Another 2 billion people rely on biomass or kerosene for cooking, lighting, and heating. The "energy poor" must spend a disproportionate amount of time and money (10-30% of household income) on sub-standard cooking and lighting fuels. They may walk for miles to find the nearest kerosene supplier or scavenge their surroundings for wood, crops, or dung to burn. All while subjecting themselves to open flames that pose health hazards to those around them. Altogether, the smoke from these cooking and lighting fuel sources has been linked to over 1.5 million deaths each year. That's more than malaria.



This has enormous consequences for society. A community without electricity cannot run computing services, refrigerate medicines, power industrial machinery, or access information from the internet. The digital divide only exacerbates disparities. While there is still no one-size-fits-all resource that can universally meet the worlds energy needs, there is a particular opportunity for solar-based solutions.



Take, for example, this map of the world at night. Due to light pollution, we can get a sense for which regions are electrified and which are not.



But what about those areas that are dark? Does this mean that these are sparsely populated regions? Hardly. Just check out this map of population density in Africa, overlaid on top of the map of electrification.



So what is the role of solar? Well, if we overlay a map of solar irradiation on top of the first maps of energy density, we can see that solar energy has a real chance to make a difference.







Solar Lanterns for Off-Grid Communities


In partnership with Princeton University's Woodrow Wilson School of Public Policy, I spent a year conducting research on the different factors that affect the adoption of solar technology - in this case, solar-powered lanterns - in a rural community in Kenya.

The Mpala Research Center in Laikipia, Kenya

With the help of some fantastic translators, I administered a household-level survey in January 2010 to the 52 families in a staff village at the Mpala Research Center. These households were a bit unique in that at least one member had to work full-time at the Center. But it still took over an hour to drive to the nearest town over rugged and unpaved roads, meaning that they shared many of the same challenges as other remote communities in the developing world.

Surveying the local community

The average wage was roughly $4/day and the majority of villagers relied on hurricane-style kerosene lanterns, often spending $5-6 on kerosene for lighting and cooking every month. The village is completely disconnected from the national energy grid, though a handful of households were able to invest in solar panels to power radios, TVs, and the like. The rest relied on 12V car batteries, if anything, which needed to be charged by third-party generators a few times a week.

Trying to study with a kerosene hurricane lantern
A typical home at the research centre

A single bulb lights up a home

Before leaving the village, I introduced a solar lantern that was built in Shanghai, China by Roy Solar. These lanterns could power a 28-pc LED lantern for 6-12 hours on a single 10 hour charge using either a solar panel or DC charger. The lanterns also included an adapter for mobile phone batteries.


After teaching the community about these lanterns, each household had the option of purchasing the lanterns, at cost ($52). Within one month, our entire initial order of 100 lanterns had been sold to the community and members of surrounding villages. With the re-couped costs, we were then able to place a second order for lanterns to meet the growing demand as their reputation spread.



In January 2011, I returned to Mpala to conduct a follow-up survey. I discovered that the lanterns had gone through quite a bit of stress and were not holding up as well as we had hoped. Fortunately, we were able to supply a limited number of spare parts (purchased in advance at the community's request) and train several members of the community to fix the lanterns accordingly.

The solar lantern after 1 year of use

Despite the villagers' frustrations with the durability of the lanterns, the overall impact was quite positive. The majority of respondents indicated that they were happy with their purchases and many were very aware of the economic and health benefits of the lanterns to their families. Many requested a second lantern and our second order of 100 lanterns sold out while I was there.

A comparison of the solar lanterns against a hurricane lantern

Ultimately, we calculated that the lanterns had a 96% adoption rate in the community, leading to a 15% drop in annual lighting-related expenditures - including a 71% drop in kerosene expenditures alone. Financing was cited as crucial to enabling technology adoption in the community, but breakage was a very real issue due to the potent combination of environmental stress and poor quality control. We were determined to develop a sustainable model to transfer the purchasing ability to the local community and eliminate me as the 'middle-man.' Unfortunately, however, the project funding ran out before the new purchasing channels could be adopted.



The Camel Fridge Project
OFF-GRID SOLAR |TECHNOLOGY FOR DEVELOPING REGIONS




CAMELS THAT SAVE LIVES



Medical treatment in remote regions presents a particular variety of challenges endemic to the surroundings. Rather than setting up permanent clinics to serve an entire region, many health workers have found that it is far more effective to operate mobile clinics that travel around the area administering health care.

Mobile health clinics must serve entire regions of nomadic communities

Because of a variety of circumstances (practicality, terrain, infrastructure, cost), these journeys may have to be taken by foot or beast of burden, rather than 4x4. This extends journeys considerably, preventing clinics from bringing little more than dry penicillin with them.

Assembling the bamboo saddle and vaccine transport delivery system

Vaccines, which need to be kept around 2-8 C have little chance of surviving the journey. However, statistics speak for the true need in these communities for vaccines and improved healthcare.As part of Princeton University's Global Development Network, we collaborated with the Pasadena School of Design to design a system that would enable these medical caravans to transport temperature-sensitive medicines on their journeys. Our prototypes were first assembled at Princeton and then tested at the Bronx Zoo, before field testing in Afar, Ethiopia and Mpala, Kenya.

Fielding testing the system in Afar, Ethiopia and Mpala, Kenya

We used a small refrigerator, similar to a standard cooler, to hold the medicines. In order to maintain the correct temperature, this refrigerator was connected to a deep-cycle battery that would be charged using solar panels thus providing a renewable energy source that would ensure constant cooling in regions of abundant sunshine such as sub-Saharan Africa. The battery was chosen with enough reserve capacity to ensure that the unit would remain cold even if there were 2-3 days without sun. All of this was integrated into a saddle that could be fitted onto a typical beast of burden in this case, a camel for convenient transport.The first generation saddle (March, 2009) was built out of bamboo, specifically for our project to integrate all of these components. The second generation, deployed in March 2010, used flexible solar panels and was designed to fit onto locally-designed camel saddles.



Phones with Soul
Mobile phones tailored to the African market




A brief glimpse into the mobile handset industry



Based on my experience in emerging markets and research involving numerous user studies with Android applications, I was asked to provide technical insights to a start-up mobile phone company, Soul, that was trying to enter the African market. This was a different kind of company, one whose genesis was centered around the unique passions, environments, and needs of their customers.

Sample logo from a basic feature phone

Soul wasn't trying to directly compete with the top brands like Apple or Samsung. But its founder saw that, particularly fueled by the accessibility of Android, a plethora of "Tier 2" companies were exceedingly popular in many of these markets because their price points were attainable. This was despite the fact that these phones were often of poor build quality and the customers themselves may have preferred "original" models if they could afford them.

Feature phone samples

Many of these Tier 2 phones were manufactured through OEM suppliers in China. This helps explain why so many phones look so similar across brands. Soul therefore set out to differentiate itself through quality packaging and materials, software customizations, and marketing campaigns. The company first entered Ghana with "generation 0" feature phones to establish a footing in the key supply chains of the country. The next step was to introduce a line of Android smartphones, each with a different theme: in addition to the basic Soul Talk and music-centric Soul Symphony feature phones, the next generation of smartphones would include the Soul Control for business users and the flagship Soul Mate.

Customized packaging for the Generation 0 feature phones

My role was to create a list of technical features to include in each design. Because we were a small company, we resignedly realized that we would initally be bound to pre-determined OEM configurations because we couldn't meet minimum order quantities (MOQs) for fully customizable options. I attended a few trade shows in Hong Kong and Shenzhen with the founder and we spoke to a number of suppliers. We compared chipsets, hardware configurations, materials, designs, and more. In order to maximize our identity, we also researched software apps, language support, ringtones, sticker/bracelet campaigns, and packaging designs.


We were ready to pull the trigger on the order.. until Ghana abruptly introduced a new series of import taxes that would have significantly increased our costs and would have forced us to sell at a loss in order to be competitive. As a new company, we simply couldn't afford the risk on the investment and reluctantly, we had to pull out. But the company and concept still remain close to our hearts.. and souls.. to this day.



UNIDO
The United Nations Industrial Development Organization




Sustainable Energy For All





In 2012, I was able to fulfill a childhood dream when I was given the opportunity to work with the United Nations on a five-month internship. But rather than joining the peacekeeping force as I had always imagined, I was an energy adviser assigned to the UN Industrial Development Organization (UNIDO)'s mission to the General Assembly in New York. This opportunity was the perfect way to cap a six-year journey into sustainable development during my graduate studies.


While I was at UNIDO, one of our chief platforms was resource efficient industrialization. As a result, we were involved in a number of initiatives trying to promote clean and sustainable energy sources.


Namely, we were heavily involved with the UN's Sustainable Energy For All (SE4All) initiative that sought to work with a variety of public, private, and civilian stakeholders to collectively advance sustainable energy use. This allowed me to participate in a number of meetings, calls, and conferences that gathered key representatives from all sectors. Although my role was more observatory, it gave me a glimpse into the very heart of Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs).


My other major task was to observe negotiations behind the formation of the Outcome Document for the impending Rio+20 Conference on Sustainable Development, which was essentially interpreted as a global progress report on goals that had been established at the original conference in Rio in 1992. The formation of this outcome document represented a global consensus on the key development challenges of the time, and would eventually lay the groundwork for the 2015 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). With these goals established, countries and companies alike would have an increased mandate to invest, partner, and pursue necessary resources to addresses these issues.


Again, my role in these meetings was purely observatory. But it granted me invaluable access to high-level negotiations between the diplomatic corps of every nation in the UN. As can be expected with so many stakeholders, who all bring together a different set of resources, needs, and constraints, the negotiations were very long and drawn out. I began to appreciate the diplomatic process after hours would be spent debating the inclusion of specific words or prepositions. But I also understood that these meetings, even if largely symbolic at times, played a very particular role in global politics.

Revitalizing landfill communities through renewable income-generating opportunities




From Wasteland to Homeland



The last century witnessed an unprecedented explosion in world population. Rising from approximately 1.6 billion people in 1900 to 6.8 billion people in 2010, this population increase has not been evenly divided geographically or socioeconomically. Furthermore, population growth is becoming increasingly concentrated in urban areas over time. The practical implications of this growth are immense: While wealthy developed nations have been able to finance the necessary infrastructure to accept growing populations, extremely poor nations are rarely in a position to do so. Even still, economic opportunities in rural areas are extremely limited and people continue to flock to the slums of the megacities in search of higher wages and access to water, food, and energy supplies.

Waste landfill communities on the outskirts of Karachi

Urban slums face a wide range of challenges due to their unique set of circumstances. The intersection of excessive population density with the lack of necessary infrastructure leads to extreme waste production that has nowhere to go but giant waste dumps at the fringes of the city limits. Many people, desperate to make a living, resort to scavenging in these sites for recyclable materials (metals, plastics, glass, etc.) that can be sold to wholesalers. Allowing organic material to sit and rot has its own negative health and environmental effects. but in order to make scavenging easier, the trash is frequently set on fire to eliminate the organic material from the co-mingled recyclables. Respiratory infections, illness, loss of sense of smell, and frequent burns to the hands and feet are commonly seen in these communities.

The waste continues to pile up and burn every day

In 2008, I was part of a team of Princeton and Rutgers students who wanted to find environmentally and financially-sustainable solutions for the waste scavengers who lived and worked in these sites. Originally, we wanted to work with a community in Karachi, Pakistan to build a waste-to-energy biodigester that could be used to electrify the community. Given the intense frequency of power outages throughout the city at the time, we envisioned that Phase 2 would see the creation of a full-fledged power plant that would sell electricity to the city at large. We would incorporate the local community members at every level of the labor force for this power plant and profits would be used for community development. Given that we would be mitigating methane emissions from these sites, we could even tap into carbon credits to further fund our system. We had it all worked out!


Well, we certainly had the idealism of university students looking to change the world. We had a lot of great ideas and in the process, grew from a student group to a 501(c)3 non-profit. We made connections with students in Pakistan who were exceptionally eager to raise awareness and help with our ground operations. We worked with other NGOs in the area to conduct needs assessments and deliver small-scale supplies to the community. We conducted art courses at a local school on the site that had been established with help from the Japanese embassy, and held fundraisers exhibiting the paintings to raise awareness and funds. We entered - and won - several business plan competitions. We rode on the coattails of enthusiasm, excitement, and idealism. We were, in short, empowered.

Art programs at the community school in Karachi

But eventually, after over 2 years of trying to work with the community in Karachi, we admitted to ourselves that the political landscape made it too challenging to continue. We found a similar community in Dhaka and turned our attention there. And then, we pivoted a second time, focusing on compost - rather than electricity - generation. We built a waste-sorting area that would be safer and more comfortabe, while still allowing the community members to generate income from the composted organic waste.


I left em[POWER] in 2011, just as the Dhaka project was getting off the ground, because I had to focus on writing up my thesis and finishing my PhD. em[POWER] has pivoted and expanded its projects several times since then, and continues to work with communities all around the world. More information can be found on their website.
